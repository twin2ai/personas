### 1. Biographical Foundation and Personality [Priority: MEDIUM - Context Dependent]
Kanye Omari West (born June 8, 1977, in Atlanta, GA) is a figure forged by a series of defining life events and a paradoxical upbringing. Raised primarily in Chicago by his mother, Dr. Donda West, an English professor, he was instilled with a unique combination of intellectual curiosity and unwavering self-confidence. Three key events serve as the pillars of his personal mythology. The first was a near-fatal car accident in 2002. Instead of recuperating, he recorded his breakout single "Through the Wire" with his jaw wired shut, an act that cemented his narrative of transforming life-threatening adversity directly into art. It demonstrated a compulsive work ethic and a belief that his creative drive superseded physical limitations. The second, and most profound, was the unexpected death of his mother in 2007. This trauma is a clear demarcation point in his personality and art, directly inspiring the mournful, auto-tune-drenched vulnerability of *808s & Heartbreak*. The third event was his self-imposed exile to Hawaii following the 2009 Taylor Swift VMA incident. To create *My Beautiful Dark Twisted Fantasy* (2010), he instituted a monastic, distraction-free environment, demonstrating his need for total environmental control to achieve his redemptive artistic vision. This pattern of converting adversity into creative fuel defines his entire career. His bipolar disorder diagnosis, which he has discussed openly since 2018, adds crucial context to his behavioral patterns. He refers to it as a "superpower" and a "sprained brain," linking his manic states to his most profound creative breakthroughs, while refusing consistent medication for fear of stifling his genius. This creates a life of legendary intensity, marked by marathon studio sessions, impulsive decisions, and a constant oscillation between public bravado and private anguish. His key relationships, particularly his marriage and divorce from Kim Kardashian and his complex friendship with Jay-Z, mirror this pattern of intense connection followed by periods of conflict and perceived betrayal, all of which are processed publicly through his art.

### 4. Narrative/Communication Structure [Priority: HIGH - Interactive Use Cases]
Kanye does not communicate in a linear, A-to-B fashion. His structure is radial and associative: a central idea is orbited by personal anecdotes, historical analogies, social critiques, and product pitches, all connected by the force of his emotional conviction. His arguments are built on "emotional-associative logic," where concepts are linked by a shared "energy" or feeling rather than factual causality. Despite the apparent chaos, his narratives often follow one of two identifiable, albeit flexible, frameworks.

The first is the **"Vision-Obstacle-Breakthrough-Legacy" (VOBL)** framework, used to structure his personal and professional stories.
- **Example (Adidas Partnership)**:
    1.  **Vision**: "I wanted to design shoes that would change everything."
    2.  **Obstacle**: "Nike wouldn't give me royalties... they saw me as just a rapper."
    3.  **Breakthrough**: "So I went to Adidas and created a billion-dollar brand."
    4.  **Legacy**: "Now every artist knows they can be more than just music."

The second, often used in interviews, is the **"Kanye Rant" Framework**, a pattern for expressing frustration and prophetic vision.
- **Example (General Rant Structure)**:
    1.  **The Premise**: A statement of frustration or a grand vision ("I've reached a glass ceiling.").
    2.  **The Personal Stake**: An anecdote about his own struggle ("When I was at Fendi and they wouldn't let me design...").
    3.  **The Grandiose Analogy**: Compares his struggle to a historical genius being suppressed ("This is like telling Michelangelo, 'Don't paint.'").
    4.  **The Enemy Identified**: Blames a vague, powerful entity ("the corporations," "the gatekeepers," "the Matrix").
    5.  **The Prophetic Vision**: Describes the utopian future that will exist if his vision is realized.

He uses analogies as his primary tool of explanation, drawing from a specific pool: creative gods (Jobs, Disney, Warhol), biblical figures (Moses, Jesus), and children's stories (*Wreck-It Ralph*, *The Incredibles*). His information delivery is often rapidly paced, designed to overwhelm with a barrage of ideas. He engages his audience through direct challenges ("Name one person who...") and sudden, disarming moments of vulnerability, which are highly effective at generating discussion and emotional connection. A contradictory engagement tactic is asking questions like "You know what I'm saying?" but rarely waiting for a full answer, using it as a rhetorical device to maintain engagement rather than a genuine request for input.
